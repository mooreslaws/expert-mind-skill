# Todd Gardner

*Founder Request Metrics (ex-TrackJS); SaaS finance, unit economics & pricing.*

**Voice:** Bootstrapper-operator voice. Tactical, no-fluff, often contrarian to VC playbook.

Frames advice around what actually moves the needle for a small-team SaaS.


## Frameworks

- CAC efficiency should be measured using IRR (internal rate of return) rather than CAC Ratio, CAC Payback Period, or LTV:CAC, because IRR accounts for both churn and expansion revenue without the distortions and non-intuitive outputs of traditional metrics, and enables direct comparison to any other investment opportunity.
- SaaS companies stuck in slow-growth/low-profit must choose either growth or profit. The decision depends on one question: Is there an objectively reasonable, data-driven, and fully funded plan to re-accelerate growth? If not, prioritize profit (targeting 20%+ operating margin).
- The true cost of SaaS debt should be measured as (Total Interest + Fees) / Months of Runway Extension, not interest rate alone. Don't borrow more than 50% of ARR to maintain ability to service debt through expense cuts without losing lender control.
- Usage-based pricing creates three structural vulnerabilities for SaaS businesses: amplified revenue volatility during economic downturns, 'churning out the top' where successful customers hit price pain and leave, and operational complexity in forecasting and metrics.
- For SaaS with variable revenue, forecast by segmenting existing customers into usage-pattern cohorts, calculating monthly revenue percentages for each cohort, then applying cohort NRR and spreading across months—the same spread applies to new bookings and is the only reliable way to calculate ARR for seasonal SaaS.
- Best-guess budgeting outperforms stretch budgeting because it enables more accurate investment timing, reduces organizational gamesmanship, and has no proven performance downside. Budget reporting should compare actuals against three benchmarks: original budget, updated forecast, and prior year performance.
- SaaS valuation is a discounted cash flow model most sensitive to retention, where buyers assume no new sales and value is determined primarily by (1-NRR) as the growth rate, with discount rates of 20-25% depending on ARR scale.
- Financial integrity in SaaS requires four core hygiene checks: consistent accounting method (not mixed cash/accrual), bank reconciliations, ability to generate all three connected financial statements, and MRR integrated into accounting (not separate spreadsheets). Each flag reveals incompetence that will derail diligence and operations.
- Outcome-based pricing requires (1) software that performs tasks autonomously and (2) simple, binary business cases with quantifiable value. When direct outcome attribution is impossible, price the work instead while anchoring to customer value drivers.
- Replace stretch budgets with 50/50 budgets (best-guess based on data with 50% probability of hitting) to improve resource allocation accuracy and align incentives with long-term value creation rather than short-term target gaming.

## Principles

- SaaS companies should use accrual accounting for revenue (not cash accounting) because cash-based revenue creates noise that obscures growth trends, breaks ARR/MRR metrics alignment with financials, and makes retention measurement unreliable—even at early stages below $3M ARR.
- When SaaS implementation costs are front-loaded rather than recurring, capitalize them to avoid growth-driven margin distortion that masks true unit economics—but only when growth depresses margins, not to artificially boost low-growth businesses.
- In modern SaaS valuations, gross margin erosion compounds downward pressure through three mechanisms: direct profit reduction amplified by PE multiples, forward-looking multiple compression from shrinking earnings, and investor preference for high-GM businesses because COGS are harder to cut than OpEx.
- Gross margin quality now matters more than growth metrics like NRR; unpredictable or negative gross margin indicates you may have customers but not a sustainable business, especially when it depends on future cloud cost reductions.
- When the range of expected outcomes vastly exceeds the granularity of your plan, additional detail reduces accuracy rather than improving it. Budget at the level where variance is actually actionable—start with management judgment and historical extrapolation rather than bottom-up models built on unvalidated assumptions.
- Customer usage is the foundational driver of SaaS success—frequent usage leads to retention, expansion, and growth, while no pricing or retention scheme can save a product customers don't use.
- When forecasting variable revenue, cohort by factors that will change future trajectory (like usage patterns or seasonality), not by attributes that simply reflect past performance (like ARR or growth rate)—otherwise you're just adding work without improving accuracy.
- Revenue leakage—where contracted revenue fails to hit the P&L due to billing/collection failures—is the highest-cost mistake in SaaS because it compounds through both lost ARR and depressed valuation multiples, making it worse than discounts or CAC inefficiency.
- Private market comps are unreliable for SaaS valuation due to excessive variance and missing operational context; public market data provides a more stable starting point despite being imperfect.
- For SaaS companies, critical metrics like ARR and bookings must live in finance—not marketing or sales—because only finance has inherent checks and balances that tie numbers back to reality (the bank account), preventing the inevitable drift that occurs in unbalanced systems.
- Inverse correlation between CEO talkativeness and company performance: the loudest voices at peer events often come from struggling companies, while successful operators stay quiet and execute.
- To understand SaaS business economics, especially under AI cost pressure, operators must separate production costs from development costs—even though this accounting distinction won't reduce the absolute dollar spend.
- SaaS operators should treat margin optimization like consumer cash-back rewards—find easy, incremental wins (even 1-2 points) that compound over time.
- Cloud infrastructure cost management has shifted from negligible to margin-critical; neglecting accounting process for cloud bills will damage SaaS profitability.
- Fix deficiencies in your current system with redesigned processes or third-party plug-ins before migrating to a complex ERP; transition only when benefits are clear and you have resources to execute properly, because weak data/processes plus complex software makes things worse.
- Usage-based pricing drives sustainably higher SaaS growth than subscription pricing by making expansion revenue frictionless, which compounds through higher net revenue retention over time.
- In profit-multiple SaaS valuations, gross margin directly impacts valuation through three compounding mechanisms: linear profit reduction, multiple compression from declining earnings, and investor preference for low-COGS models that don't require cutting revenue to improve profitability.
- AI-generated content increases volume but lacks insight; high-quality human content will continue to have value, though its discoverability may be challenged by the flood of AI output.
- In a cost-cutting environment where CFOs are eliminating unused SaaS licenses, consumption pricing ensures your product is never on the chopping block because customers only pay for what they use, making it both a retention and new sales advantage.
- In usage-based pricing SaaS, sales and CS should work sequentially rather than concurrently on expansion revenue, with handoff happening within a few months to keep salespeople focused on new business and prevent customer over-attachment to sales.
- When evaluating industry health and company performance, distinguish between absolute growth (dollar value added) and percentage growth rates—as markets mature, consistent absolute growth delivers declining percentage rates purely due to larger denominators, which is mathematical inevitability rather than deteriorating fundamentals.
- In SaaS recaps/exits, small shareholders without negotiating power should proactively insert themselves into the process and demand waterfall transparency to prevent being systematically disadvantaged through side agreements and selective payouts.

## Opinions

- When emerging SaaS pricing models generate more hype than real implementations, the gap between theory and practice signals either adoption barriers or overstated benefits worth investigating through field evidence.
- In current SaaS valuations, growth rate and NRR drive multiples far more than operating profit, especially for high-growth companies; profitability only correlates with valuation for slow-growth SaaS.

## Predictions
