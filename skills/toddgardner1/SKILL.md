## Frameworks

- SaaS loan cost-effectiveness should be measured by (Total Interest + Fees) / Months of Runway Extension, not interest rate. Don't borrow more than 50% of ARR to maintain the ability to service debt through expense cuts without losing lender control.
- CAC efficiency should be measured using IRR (Internal Rate of Return) rather than CAC Ratio, CAC Payback Period, or LTV to CAC, because IRR accounts for both retention and expansion revenue properly while producing an intuitive metric comparable to any other investment.
- Pick a lane: growth or profit. SaaS companies must choose based on whether they have an objectively reasonable, data-driven, and fully funded plan to re-accelerate growth. If no such plan exists, target 20%+ operating profit immediately rather than lingering in slow-growth/low-profit limbo.
- For SaaS with variable revenue, forecast by segmenting existing customers into usage-based cohorts, spreading each cohort's revenue as monthly percentages, then multiply by NRR and apply the same spread to new bookings.
- Four financial red flags validate SaaS accounting integrity: (1) mixed cash/accrual methods, (2) missing bank reconciliations, (3) inability to generate connected three-statement financials, and (4) disconnected MRR reporting.
- Usage-based pricing creates three structural vulnerabilities: (1) revenue contracts faster than subscriptions in economic downturns, (2) successful expansion can trigger 'churning out the top' when bills grow exponentially, and (3) variable revenue makes forecasting and ARR calculation inherently problematic.
- Outcome-based pricing requires two conditions: (1) software that performs tasks autonomously and (2) business cases that are simple and binary. When attribution is too complex, pivot to work-based pricing anchored to customer value drivers instead.
- Replace stretch budgets with 50/50 budgets (best-guess based on data with 50% probability of hitting) to enable accurate resource allocation and avoid distorted short-term incentives; tie compensation to year-over-year gains rather than budget attainment.
- AI pricing decisions should follow a value-visibility dichotomy: if AI value-add is immediate/obvious/material, monetize it; if not, include it free to support retention. Within paid AI, consumption pricing beats subscription because it links cost to value.
- Credit failures stem from two distinct mechanisms: structural (asset-liability mismatch enabling panic) versus fundamental (loan quality). Even sound loans fail if capital can be withdrawn faster than portfolio liquidation, making fund structure as critical as underwriting.

## Principles

- SaaS companies should use accrual accounting for revenue because cash-based revenue creates noise that obscures ARR tracking, distorts retention metrics, and makes growth trajectories harder to gauge.
- Valuation is most sensitive to retention and gross margin; buyers model downside by assuming zero new sales and conservative retention, so sellers should build their own DCF model with 20-25% discount rates and challenge buyer assumptions.
- Maximize SaaS exit value by aligning three vectors: owner liquidity preferences, company performance timing (avoiding peak booking/renewal periods, conservative forecasts), and external market conditions (avoiding valleys, not waiting for outlier peaks).
- Stop obsessing over valuation multiples. ARR growth typically creates more absolute value than multiple expansion, and must be weighed against downside 'bad stuff' risk that can simultaneously depress both ARR and multiples.
- SaaS valuations depend more on credible future narratives than current metrics (Rule of 40 explains <50% of valuation); sellers should time exits after proving near-term revenue and eliminate downside risk through locked-in contracts.
- In high-growth SaaS with front-loaded implementation costs, capitalize onboarding expenses to reveal true unit economics; without it, new customer growth artificially depresses gross margins and obscures viable business models.
- Gross margin has displaced NRR as the key health metric for SaaS; unpredictable gross margin indicates customer traction without a real business, especially when dependent on uncertain future cloud cost reductions.
- When the range of expected outcomes vastly exceeds the granularity of the plan, adding detail to budgets wastes time and creates false precision. Start with top-down judgment using historical results rather than bottom-up projections.
- In modern SaaS valuations, gross margin changes directly multiply through to valuation impact because they flow straight to operating profit, declining margins compress P/E ratios, and COGS are harder to cut than opex.
- Usage is the primary driver of SaaS success—if customers use your product frequently, you'll figure out monetization; if they don't, no pricing or retention scheme will save you.
- Cohorts for revenue forecasting should be built around behavioral/usage patterns that predict different trajectories, not static attributes like ARR or historical growth rate.
- In usage-based pricing SaaS, hand off customers from sales to CS as early as possible (within a few months) to free sales capacity for new prospects and prevent customers from becoming attached to the salesperson.
- Book-closing speed is a leading indicator of company quality: sub-$3M ARR should close by the 15th, $5M+ within a week. Fast close signals data-driven management, financial predictability, and lower fraud risk.
- Revenue leakage—revenue you've earned contractually but failed to bill—compounds catastrophically because it destroys ARR, profit margins, growth rate, and valuation multiples simultaneously, making it more damaging than direct cash burn.
- In SaaS, operational data (ARR, bookings) must live in finance and reconcile to cash, not in marketing spreadsheets, because only finance has inherent checks and balances that tie to reality.
- To understand SaaS unit economics under AI cost pressure, you must separately track production costs from development costs—conflating them obscures your true margin picture.
- Don't upgrade core systems (like ERP) until the ROI is clear and you have capacity to execute properly; fix what you can with current tools and plugins first, because premature or poorly-executed migrations can destroy companies.
- Consumption pricing protects SaaS products from budget cuts because CFOs prioritize eliminating unused seat licenses first; zero underutilization means you're never on the chopping block at renewal time.
- When evaluating industry health, look past growth rate percentages to absolute dollar growth; as industries mature, consistent dollar growth necessarily produces declining percentage rates due to base effects.
- When selling slower-growth SaaS businesses, run a broad competitive auction process to capture the surprisingly wide valuation range (1x to 4x revenue) that different financial buyers will offer for identical businesses.
- For non-core AI integrations in SaaS, deliberate execution beats speed-to-market because skeptical buyers reward genuine utility over AI-washing, and usage-based pricing complexity requires upfront architectural decisions.
- Back-office SaaS (ERP, HR, DevOps) retains customers better during downturns than front-office applications (sales, marketing) because companies cannot cancel mission-critical systems but easily drop marginal marketing tools.

## Opinions

- For bootstrapped founders, macro economic conditions and market timing debates are uncontrollable distractions; execution matters more than whether conditions are optimal or a 'bubble' exists.

- SaaS valuations haven't fundamentally changed from historical norms (7-8X revenue); what shifted is the financial profile—companies now trade similar multiples but with half the growth and significantly higher profitability.

- AI's threat to software companies mirrors open-source's historical impact: while code replication becomes cheaper, competitive advantage lies in ancillary services that free or AI-generated code cannot match.
- Usage-based pricing models amplify both growth and contraction because they have virtually zero friction to revenue changes, making them more volatile in both boom and downturn cycles.

## Predictions

- Usage-based pricing fits products with four characteristics: high COGS (infrastructure/AI with real costs), revenue linkage (usage correlates with customer revenue), self-provisioning/PLG compatibility, and unbound upside.
- SaaS valuations have shifted from revenue multiples to cash flow multiples; early-stage companies must demonstrate efficiency in both their 'growth engine' (CAC ratio, burn ratio) and 'cash engine' (gross margin, net retention).
- Revenue Leakage occurs when billing complexity (especially usage-based pricing) causes unbilled/underbilled invoices, destroying profit through known system errors or unknown opaque processes.
- Market capital has a natural floor set by maintenance investment in existing portfolios, which protects incumbents but does not translate to healthier conditions for new entrants.
- For SaaS valuations, growth rate and NRR now dominate over operating profit, but the importance of profit increases as growth slows—meaning efficient growth always matters even when efficiency itself isn't currently valued.
- When evaluating SaaS market conditions, distinguish between declining growth rates versus declining absolute spending, and recognize that market saturation naturally compresses growth even in healthy industries.
- Labor productivity improvements drive non-inflationary economic growth, and generative AI's direct impact on white-collar work represents a uniquely transformational lever for companies with large knowledge worker bases.
- The slowdown in SaaS did not begin in 2022—it started almost ten years ago. Annual revenue growth in early 2015 was 42%; as of the most recent quarter, it was just 17%.
