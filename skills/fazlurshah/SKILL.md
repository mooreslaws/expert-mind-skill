## Frameworks

- Profitability should be analyzed through layered contribution margins (CM1: product economics, CM2: sustainable growth, CM3: unit profitability) to identify which operational levers to pull for optimization.
- An effective one-minute startup pitch should follow a structured template that covers pain point, market size, solution, differentiator, team credibility, business model, analogy, vision, traction, and personalized invitation—in that specific order to maximize comprehension and engagement.
- For early-stage fundraising, size your target follower check by dividing remaining round size by conversation threshold and hit rate, which determines both check size and investor count needed to close the round.
- Cash Conversion Score (CCS = Current Revenue / Total Cash Burned) measures capital efficiency; scores ≥1.0 indicate sustainable growth, while lower scores signal unsustainable burn requiring intervention through pricing fixes, marketing cost reduction, retention focus, expense cuts, and faster cash collection.
- When calculating market penetration for venture returns, use Bottom-Up TAM (potential customers × realistic ACV) rather than Top-Down (revenue/total TAM), because revenue concentration (80/20 rule) means average customer value is far lower than implied by dividing total TAM by customer count.
- Minor triggers often cause disproportionate harm through our exaggerated reactions rather than the trigger itself. Combat this by naming the actual threat level, applying the 5-5-5 time perspective rule, and using physiological interrupts to prevent emotional escalation.
- A startup cap table becomes uninvestable when founders are over-diluted (should own >50% post-Series A, ~70% post-Seed), lack vesting schedules, have too many inactive shareholders, or carry excessive protective provisions that discourage new investors.
- Markets shift from local/referral-based networks to algorithmic, data-driven discovery systems as technology reduces search costs and eliminates geographic constraints—a pattern that applies to both dating and business customer acquisition.

## Principles

- Fundraising success comes from high-velocity iteration through rejection cycles rather than perfecting pitch materials. Every 'no' teaches you something: which slide made them check their phone, what number made them lean forward, why they passed (the real reason, not the polite one), who they think would be perfect instead.
- Managing strict investors requires proactive communication, transparency, and strategic problem-solving through structured updates, preemptive problem disclosure, realistic expectation-setting, and alignment with VC incentives.
- CAC payback period must be calculated using gross margin, not top-line revenue, to avoid systematically underestimating the time required to recover customer acquisition costs and potentially running out of runway.
- In fundraising, founders should evaluate deals by their post-dilution ownership value rather than headline pre-money valuation, as large post-money option pools dilute founders before investors enter.
- VCs prefer option pools created pre-investment rather than post-investment because it preserves their ownership percentage while diluting founders proportionally, even though founder absolute percentages appear unchanged.
- Board control is determined by seat allocation, not equity percentage; founders can lose decision-making power despite holding majority ownership if they're outvoted in the boardroom.
- When entering crowded markets, focus on paradigm-shifting differentiation rather than competing on incumbent metrics; market leaders often can't see category innovation because they're optimizing for current standards.
- In startups, inaction while waiting for perfect conditions is riskier than taking action despite uncertainty, because standing still exposes you to competitive and market threats while movement enables learning and progress.
- When assessing VC average check size, work backwards from fund size by sequentially netting out management fees, follow-on reserves, and follow-on ratios to arrive at the true initial investment capacity.
- ESOPs represent paper wealth with real risk—understand vesting structures (especially back-loaded schedules), buyback rights, and termination clauses before valuing them, since leaving early can eliminate most of your upside.
- Founders should carefully evaluate equity distribution to investors based on their ongoing engagement and value-add potential, not just initial contributions, as inactive investors create 'dead equity' without providing operational support or decision-making participation.
- When structuring ESOP offers for key hires, calculate the current value as shares × (current price - strike price) and express it as a percentage of base salary to assess competitiveness; model future value accounting for dilution to show upside potential.
- In startup evaluation, management teams that under-promise and let financial execution speak (shrinking losses, expanding margins) are more credible than those offering aggressive profitability guidance and hype, especially when operating under structural disadvantages.
- Leverage beats ownership; control assets through creative arrangements (leasing, OPM) rather than capital-intensive purchases, especially when starting resource-constrained ventures.
- When using AI tools, human users retain final responsibility for outcomes and must independently verify outputs before acting on them.
- When preparing fundraising ask slides, break down the total raise into specific budget allocations with clear fund utilization details and measurable milestones for each category to demonstrate disciplined capital deployment.

## Opinions

If you show revenue, people will ask how much, and it will never be enough. But if you have no revenue, you can say you're pre-revenue—a potential pure play.
