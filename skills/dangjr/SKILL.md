## Frameworks

- Antipatterns in venture capital are superficially appealing heuristics that destroy performance by trading 'uncertain but right' for 'confident and wrong'—three key antipatterns (GP profile overweighting, early performance metrics worship, and founder NPS focus) offer short-term comfort but weaken long-term results.
- Venture capital operates in predictable boom-bust cycles driven by interest rates and sentiment, where low rates → risk-seeking → overvaluation → contraction → profitability focus → reset. The cycle's illiquidity and momentum amplify both inflation and crash phases.
- In opaque environments, confidence (storytelling, bias exploitation) displaces competence (measurable skill) as the basis for evaluation and reward, creating vulnerability to conmen who optimize for narrative over substance.
- IPOs represent a direct trade of transparency for lower cost of capital. When interest rates are low, companies stay private longer producing paper gains, but this suppresses quality of eventual IPOs while inflating late-stage VC valuations.
- Large venture funds scale allocation by fundamentally redefining risk: they compress idiosyncratic risk through consensus-building and leverage systematic risk momentum, essentially financializing venture capital.
- The 'kingmaking fallacy' occurs when investors mistakenly believe raising large capital causes success, when reality shows capital efficiency drives innovation and excess funding creates inflated but weaker outcomes ('hollow giants').
- VC funding has shifted from identifying outliers to coordinating consensus around 'fundability'—the ability to attract follow-on capital—which creates self-reinforcing concentration and kills alpha generation.
- Venture capital cycles create systematic risk by confusing price (investor demand signal) with valuation (true worth for exits). The recursive funding-growth-multiple loop drives exponential swings: more funding → faster growth → multiple expansion → higher prices → more funding.
- Early-stage investing fails when it overindexes on legible founder credentials through the Great Man Fallacy and Overfitting, rather than evaluating the unique combination of entrepreneur and problem. The atomic unit of entrepreneurship is the alchemical mix of founder and idea together.
- A 'risk bubble' emerges when capital floods illiquid assets not from optimism about fundamentals, but from VCs acting as momentum traders chasing short-term performance illusion while anticipating market consolidation.

## Principles

- In venture capital ecosystems, state intervention should prioritize early-stage (pre-seed/angel) funding over growth capital, as upstream capital allocation creates more capital-efficient productivity gains (2x), generates downstream deal flow organically, and resolves the 'inception capital gap' that constrains later-stage markets.
- Capital allocation in venture requires consistent funding across market cycles because (1) herd behavior in timing creates systemic damage through overconcentration and no-invest equilibria, and (2) good vintages are unpredictable, making market timing counterproductive.
- If the greatest entrepreneurs are outliers, then investing based on patterns and credentials is irrelevant; true venture success requires rejecting conventional screening methods despite higher operational costs.
- There exists an optimal funding amount relative to stage that maximizes success; both over-funding and under-funding increase failure risk, with over-funding creating rigidity through headcount and preventing rapid iteration needed to find product-market fit.
- When a theme becomes hot enough to attract specialized funds, the best opportunities have likely already emerged; the theme's visibility is a lagging indicator of opportunity exhaustion.
- Venture returns come from managing idiosyncratic risk in frontier technology, not from following herd consensus into hot categories where unique risk has been eliminated.
- Capital deployment constraints are misattributed to founder scarcity when the actual bottleneck is investor competence; the ceiling on competent investors is reached before any ceiling on great founders or available capital.
- VCs optimize for fundraising ability rather than founder quality because it makes their job easier and helps deploy capital, which erodes returns by missing great founders who don't fit the archetype.
- Venture capital structurally fails the most innovative ideas because clustering around popular categories reduces information friction, while novel technologies create valuation challenges that disadvantage them despite their importance.
- In power-law distributed outcomes where winners can't be reliably predicted, maximizing portfolio diversification (50+ investments vs 25-30) dramatically improves fund survival odds and returns—venture capital is a portfolio discipline, not a picking contest.
- Excessive early-stage funding inversely correlates with startup success by causing premature scaling, lost focus, and reduced urgency—capital acts like a performance-enhancing drug that's toxic in excess.
- Venture capital success depends solely on outlier return potential (50-100x over 10 years), not industry category, stage, location, or founder attributes—investors will fund anything that can credibly demonstrate this potential.
- VCs systematically overweight subjective founder attributes (gender, elite credentials, geography) over predictive business fundamentals, especially in hot markets; combat this by redesigning evaluation processes to deprioritize founder characteristic assessment and remembering that outliers are inherently differentiated from patterns.
- In VC, improving deal flow (pool of opportunities) yields better returns than improving selection skill, because even modest expansion of opportunities mathematically outweighs major improvements in picking ability.
- Cold inbound deal flow, though often neglected, provides stronger venture returns with less capital than warm referrals because it forces pattern-based screening free from relationship bias.
- Policy should incentivize growth at the base layer through early-stage capital and founder incentives, rather than extracting value through punitive exit taxes—increasing tax revenue comes from supporting innovation to grow the pie, not squeezing a shrinking one harder.
- Early-stage venture firms can achieve exceptional returns by targeting geographies outside competitive hubs where signal is clearer and deal heat is lower.
- VCs invest more readily in legible ideas, creating a feedback loop where founders pattern-match rather than innovate; making novel ideas more understandable can reverse this dynamic and accelerate progress.
- In venture-backed IPOs, VCs prioritize quick exits at maximum price while founders think long-term, creating misaligned incentives that lead to systematic underpricing where VCs' interests align more with underwriters than with founders.
- Sustainable tech ecosystem growth requires operational knowledge transfer from successful founders to new ones, combined with equity compensation structures that redistribute wealth back into the ecosystem as new ventures and angel capital.
- Vesting schedules should align with actual time-to-liquidity horizons and equity amounts, not historical standards from different market eras; as exit timelines have doubled from ~4 years to ~8 years, founder vesting should extend accordingly while scaling employee vesting based on equity granted and role criticality.
- Portfolio construction decisions (diversification) should counterbalance systematic VC judgment failures (overconfidence, herd behavior) rather than being subordinated to narrative appeal.

## Opinions

- VCs systematically inflate portfolio valuations before fundraising through reciprocal overpricing with syndicate partners, creating an 'n-curve' pattern where performance peaks during fundraising then declines afterward, explaining why interim metrics poorly predict long-term returns.
- Capital shortages at later stages are symptoms of underinvestment at the earliest stages; fixing growth-stage funding without addressing pre-seed/angel gaps will weaken returns by funding underperformers rather than creating real opportunity.
- Good investors manage risk through portfolio construction and metrics like Sharpe ratios; bad investors rely on overconfidence and simple narratives, leading to unnecessary volatility and underperformance.
- Technology companies are trapped in a rent-extraction phase instead of Perez's 'installation phase' where benefits spread economy-wide; structurally bloated VC fund economics prevent financing ideas that proliferate social benefits, driving toward socialist backlash.
- VC focus on ARR and short-term markups creates misalignment between fund performance metrics and underlying business quality, using proxy metrics that don't correlate with actual long-term potential.
- When raising multiple SAFE rounds, increase the cap by at least the capital raised in previous SAFEs plus additional growth premium to fairly account for value accreted and protect earlier investors.
- In fundraising, a sufficiently powerful story trumps other details; what matters most isn't reaching baseline competence but achieving differentiation through missionary-level conviction.
- Financial engineering that prioritizes asset-light metrics and short-term gains over R&D and manufacturing capacity leads to operational degradation and competitive vulnerability, even for market leaders.
- AI-lowered barriers to startup surface quality force investors to shift from pattern matching on presentation to rigorous evaluation of financial fundamentals and founder competence.
- VCs avoid financial rigor by hiding behind power law dynamics and herd behavior, but alpha requires understanding specific business value drivers rather than relying on crude comparative pricing in an asset class defined by outliers.

## Predictions

- To overcome capital misallocation in deep tech and escape financialization, implement a three-layer 'national capitalism' framework where government acts as: (1) LP backing distributed small funds, (2) venture philanthropist for university spinouts, and (3) guarantor of securitized megafunds for high-risk R&D.
- Venture capital firms inflate existential narratives (like AI superintelligence threats) during market downturns to continue fundraising cycles and avoid reckoning, turning innovation hype into survival mechanism—a pattern requiring eventual painful correction.
- VCs pursuing scale as primary success metric have compromised other valuable dimensions (governance, competitive position, economics), creating a 'higher bar' fallacy that degrades their relationship with public markets and prevents optimal exits.
- Private capital inflation causes poor venture liquidity: when investors delay IPOs to maximize deployment opportunities, they incentivize risky late-stage growth strategies that produce weaker public market candidates with lower profitability and higher risk.
- Peter Thiel said 'college sucks, let's find talented young adults without a degree' and crushed VC success rates. You can find generational success in venture capital if you build your thesis around funding outsiders.
- Effective venture capital policy must start with the widest possible distribution of early-stage capital, not with later-stage scale-up support, because all venture success is downstream of the industry's ability to recognize and invest in potential at the earliest stages.
- Risk bubbles in private markets manifest as saw-tooth patterns rather than quick crashes because delayed exits and capital concentration create a slow-building casino culture driven by financialisation's preference for capital accumulation over productive investment.
- Avoid investors who price rounds using ARR multiples in certain market conditions, as this approach is financially illiterate, procyclical, and exploitable; instead seek investors who are good fiduciaries for long-term support and better advice.
