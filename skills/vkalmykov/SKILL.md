## Frameworks

- Career advancement follows three distinct strategies: (1) plateauing early to minimize stress, (2) finding an optimal middle position balancing compensation and pressure, or (3) pursuing continuous advancement despite accumulating costs. Each PM promotion brings not only money but also more pain—more context shifting, Tetris-like calendars full of back-to-backs, loss of the feeling that you know your product fully, more inevitable politics (the pyramid is narrower at the top), and higher risks.
- Product work seniority follows an inverse pain-joy ratio (20%-80% junior to 80%-20% director+), driven by increasing uncertainty, negotiation complexity, and delayed dopamine cycles. A developer's dopamine cycle is hours; for a Group PM, it's 6-12 months from idea to seeing it alive.
- A good strategy document must have a concise 5-7 slide core with detailed appendix for zoom-in capability, and must open with the customer problem/opportunity rather than vision or solution to avoid solving problems that aren't worth solving.
- Effective strategy requires three components in sequence: a clear problem statement, an actionable plan with milestones, and MVP launches within 2-3 months to prove execution matches vision. A strategy gains strength through iterative challenge and refinement, with repeated objections converted into explicit answers.
- Product success requires both technical and business metrics to be healthy. If technical metrics are ok but business ones are not, you have a working product that no one needs. Track 2-3 Service Level Indicators (SLIs) as main technical metrics to monitor product health without drowning in dashboards.
- Skill acquisition requires choosing between superficial shortcuts (checklists, free courses, hacks) versus deep learning through focused commitment, protected time, sustained effort through difficulty, and practical application to form lasting neural pathways.
- A PM strategy is good only if two conditions hold: (1) people believe your story (clear problem + trusted plan), and (2) you can deliver what you promised. PMs who deliver on their strategies get promoted; those who don't become slide-builders.
- To evaluate AI-building tools from a non-technical perspective, assess them across four dimensions: Frontend/UI generation, Backend/API integration capability, Database management for production, and public deployment of full stack. This framework separates UI cloning (where tools are comparable) from true full-stack integration (where tools diverge significantly).
- Platform PM roles require three core competencies: innovation at scale (supporting hundreds of use cases), technical depth (to drive business impact), and translation ability (bridging tech and customer needs).
- Career optimization involves a trade-off dimension between mission/impact (exciting, cutting-edge, helps humanity) and compensation (salary), with a middle-ground equilibrium available.

## Principles

- Every pixel on successful apps serves a business metric; product features exist to solve customer problems that map to measurable business outcomes.
- In A/B testing, you must define and strictly adhere to your statistical significance threshold (e.g., p-value < 0.10) before running the experiment, and reject changes that fail to meet it regardless of how close they come, to avoid cherry-picking results.
- To stress-test strategies and shift from overconfidence to critical thinking, ask whether you would invest your own money in the plan—personal financial risk cuts through bias and forces realistic evaluation.
- Forcing user actions to inflate product metrics creates toxic Goodhart's law effects: the metric improves while actual customer sentiment deteriorates and data quality becomes meaningless, ultimately driving churn when alternatives emerge.
- Correlation does not imply causation; when two variables correlate, look for a common underlying driver rather than assuming a direct causal relationship between them.
- When pitching large features, use GenAI prototyping tools to create experienceable demos rather than static presentations, because they better simulate the actual product experience and help convince stakeholders.
- Technical foundations must precede AI prototyping work; attempting to build with AI without understanding underlying tech concepts (APIs, service architecture, testing) leads to ineffective outcomes.
- Convert notification-triggered cognitive loops into explicit tasks with context links to prevent repeated re-processing of the same information and reclaim attention bandwidth.
- AI output requires critical verification because aggregation layers (AI summarizing AI-generated content) create convincing but factually unreliable statements that can lead to catastrophic product decisions.
- Career security comes not from avoiding layoffs (which are inevitable for everyone including founders) but from being demonstrably top-tier in your craft—the market has abundant opportunities for those who honestly assess and develop their skills to the top 20% or better.
- Personal Development Plans (PDPs) serve three core functions: they discipline practitioners to maintain focus amid constant operational chaos, they force improvement by surfacing feedback, and they align individual and manager expectations to prevent year-end misalignment.
- Before making career-disrupting moves based on technology hype, validate the actual capabilities through hands-on investigation and small-scale experimentation—understanding foundational building blocks provides entry advantage over chasing novelty.
- When using AI-generated outputs, beware the speed-understanding tradeoff: accelerating content creation may decelerate comprehension by others, and what can be generated instantly likely doesn't need extensive documentation at all.
- AI-powered prototypes that feel real enough to critique unlock faster stakeholder alignment and earlier product challenges, accelerating development speed without changing the product lifecycle.
- Frequent short-tenure job changes (3-4 months each) signal risk to employers and create a self-reinforcing hiring barrier, as each new position makes a candidate appear less stable to the next potential employer.

- AI can accelerate strategy research and analysis, but humans must synthesize the final strategy because AI produces generic outputs that lack the sharp nuances and edges needed for leadership buy-in.
